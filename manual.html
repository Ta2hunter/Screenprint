1
What is screen printing?
The basics — explained simply

Think of screen printing like a very precise stencil system. Instead of one stencil, you use one stencil per color.

How screen printing works
Your Design
→
Separate into colors
→
Print each color on film
→
Burn screen with film
→
Print ink through screen
→
Finished garment
1
One screen per color
If your design has 4 colors — red, navy, black, white — you need 4 screens. Each screen only carries one color of ink.
2
Film negatives go on the screen
You print each color as a black film negative — black where ink should go. You then press this film against a light-sensitive screen and expose it to UV light. The light hardens the screen everywhere except where your black film was. Wash it out, and you have a stencil.
3
Print one color at a time
Put the garment on the platen (the flat board). Press screen 1 down, push ink through with a squeegee. Lift. Move to screen 2. Repeat. Each color lines up using the registration marks — the crosshair targets on every plate.
💡 Key insight This app takes your design and automatically creates each color as a separate film-ready file. You then print those files on film, burn your screens, and you're ready to print.
2
Your first print — complete walkthrough
From zero to ready-to-burn in 10 steps
📋 Before you start Have your artwork ready as a PNG or JPG file. PNG is better because it can have a transparent background. The higher resolution the better — at least 300dpi if possible.
1
Load your image
Tap the 🎨 artwork box on the left side. Pick your PNG or JPG from your iPad files. Your design appears in the center canvas.
2
Remove the background
Tap ✂️ Remove BG. This removes the white canvas around your artwork. Without this, your separation will catch dots and noise outside your design. Always do this first.
3
Set your print size
Under Canvas Size, type the width and height in cm. A standard chest print is about 28–32cm wide. The height adjusts automatically to keep proportions correct.
4
Choose your garment color
Select the garment color from the dropdown. This affects the preview — it does not change your artwork. Black garment = you need a white underbase. White garment = no underbase needed usually.
5
Run color separation
For a photographic/gradient image: tap ⚡ Auto Sep — splits into Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Black channels.

For a spot color logo/design (flat colors, like the badminton design): tap 🎨 Spot — detects each individual color separately. This is usually better for garment printing.
6
Turn off colors you're not printing
In the Channels list, each color has a 🖨 print button. Tap it to exclude colors you don't need. The button goes dim when excluded. Only your selected colors will export.
7
Apply halftone (if needed)
For photographic prints with gradients, go to the HT tab on the right and tap ⚡ Apply Halftone. For flat spot colors, you can skip this — solid areas don't need halftone dots.
8
Apply RIP for film output
Go to the RIP tab on the right. Tap ⚡ Apply RIP. This converts everything to pure black and white — maximum contrast — ready for film printing. This is what you send to your inkjet film printer.
9
Preview the result
Tap 🔬 HT Composite in the header to see all colors stacked together — this is what the final print will look like. Tap 👕 Preview to see it on a garment silhouette.
10
Export your film files
Tap ⬇ Export All in the header. Each color downloads as a separate PNG with registration marks and a plate label. Send these to your inkjet film printer. Print on clear film, burn your screens, done.

This is the physical size of your print on the garment. Type 30cm width for a standard chest print. The app calculates how many pixels that is at your chosen DPI. It does NOT stretch or change your artwork — it just tells the app how big the output file should be.

300 DPI = standard for most prints. Good quality, manageable file size.
600 DPI = for fine detail or when going through a RIP printer. Larger files.
150 DPI = quick preview only, not for final output.
C
The 🔗 link button between width and height
When the chain link is yellow/connected, changing the width automatically adjusts the height to keep your design's proportions correct. Tap it to unlock if you want to stretch the canvas independently.
4
Color separation explained
The most important concept in screen printing

Color separation means splitting your full-color design into individual single-color layers. Each layer becomes one screen.

⚡ Auto Sep — use for:
  • Photos
  • Gradients (colors that fade into each other)
  • Photorealistic artwork
  • Simulated process printing
🎨 Spot — use for:
  • Logos with flat solid colors
  • Cartoon/vector style artwork
  • Text designs
  • Anything with clear distinct colors
💡 Which is my design? Look at your artwork. Can you count the colors? (Red, navy, black, white = 4 colors) → Use Spot. Does it have smooth gradients that blend together like a photograph? → Use Auto Sep.
?
What is CMYK?
CMYK stands for Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key (Black). These are the four ink colors used in process printing. By mixing these four at different dot sizes, you can recreate almost any color — like how your home printer works. Auto Sep splits your design into these 4 channels.
?
What is a spot color?
A spot color is a pre-mixed specific ink. Instead of mixing CMYK dots to simulate red, you just use actual red ink in its own screen. Spot colors are more vibrant and accurate — but each one needs its own screen. A 4-spot-color job needs 4 screens.
?
What is Black Mode?
Knockout Black = removes all other ink colors from areas that are black. The black plate is the only thing printing in black areas. Use for DTF and dark garments.

Overprint Black = black prints on top of all other colors. Use for screen print on white garments — gives richer blacks.

Pure K = only the K (black) channel carries black ink. Best for crisp text and fine lines.
5
Halftone — what it is and why you need it
How gradients get printed with solid ink

Screen printing can only print one thing: ink or no ink. It cannot print "50% red". So to create the appearance of lighter or darker shades, we use halftone dots.

🔵 The idea Imagine a newspaper photo up close — you can see tiny dots. Far away it looks like a smooth photo. That's halftone. Big dots = dark area. Small dots = light area. No dots = white.
SettingWhat it doesRecommended
LPI (Lines Per Inch)How many rows of dots per inch. Higher = finer detail but needs finer mesh45 LPI for most jobs
ShapeCircle, square, diamond, line etc. Changes the look of the dot patternCircle for most work
AngleThe angle the dot grid is rotated. Different channels need different angles to prevent moiré patternsUse per-channel angles
ContrastPushes highlights lighter and shadows darkerStart at 20
Min/Max dotClips very small or very large dots that won't print cleanlyMin 5%, Max 95%
⚠️ Mesh count rule Your LPI must be no more than 1/3 of your mesh count. Example: 156 mesh screen = maximum 52 LPI. If your LPI is too high for your mesh, the dots will clog and the print will be muddy. The mesh guide table in the app tells you the status automatically — green = OK, red = too fine.
!
When do I NOT need halftone?
If your design is flat solid spot colors with no gradients — like a logo, text, or cartoon-style art — you don't need halftone. The solid ink areas print as solid. Skip halftone and go straight to RIP.
!
Live preview slider
The Live Preview toggle at the top of the HT tab shows a real-time low-res preview as you drag sliders. When you lift your finger the full quality version processes. Use this to dial in settings before committing.
6
RIP — creating your film negatives
The final step before burning screens

RIP stands for Raster Image Processor. It converts your artwork into the cleanest possible black and white output for printing on film.

✅ What good film output looks like Pure solid black where ink should go. Pure white everywhere else. No grey areas. No anti-aliased edges. Maximum contrast. This is what burns the sharpest screen.
SettingWhat it doesUse when
1-bit pure B&WEverything either pure black or pure white — hardest edgeSpot colors, logos, text, solid areas
Floyd-Steinberg ditherCreates a random dot pattern to simulate grey tonesPhotographic prints without halftone
Stochastic FMRandom tiny dots instead of regular grid — very fine detailDTF, high detail photographic
GrayscaleKeeps grey values — use only if your printer handles greyscale filmRarely needed
A
Invert (Ink positive)
Normally your film is black where ink goes (negative). Some exposure units want the opposite — white where ink goes. Toggle Ink positive if your printer or bureau needs this. Ask your film output provider which they need.
B
Mirror output
Some printers need the film mirrored/flipped so the ink side is face-down against the screen during exposure. If your burns come out reversed, toggle Mirror on. Check with your exposure unit manufacturer.
C
Sharpen passes
Runs an unsharp mask filter before the 1-bit conversion. 1–2 passes is usually enough to get crisp edges. Too many passes creates harsh artifacts.
7
Registration marks — your alignment system
How multi-color prints stay perfectly aligned

Registration marks are the crosshair targets that appear on every plate. They let you line up multiple screens to print in exactly the same position.

1
Every plate has the same marks in the same positions
There are 8 reg marks — one at each corner, one in the middle of each side. They are always in the exact same position on every plate. This is how you align screen 2 to match screen 1 perfectly.
2
The bottom info bar tells you which screen is which
Every exported plate has a solid black bar at the bottom with: the color name, LPI, angle, DPI, canvas size, and date. There is also a label in the top left corner. When you have 4 films on your workbench you will always know which is which.
3
How to use reg marks at the press
Tape your first film (usually the black or underbase) to the screen glass. Expose it. When setting up the press, use the reg marks as your reference points. All subsequent films should have their reg marks in exactly the same position. Use a light table or registration pins for precise alignment.
💡 Pro tip Print a test registration sheet first on paper through all your screens before committing to garments. Hold it up to the light — all the reg marks should stack perfectly on top of each other.
8
Underbase and choke — printing on dark garments
Why you need white under everything
⚠️ Dark garment rule If you print colored ink directly onto a black or dark garment, the garment color shows through and your colors look dull and wrong. You must print white ink first as a base layer. This is the underbase.
1
Enable white underbase
In the left panel under Underbase / Choke, toggle White underbase on. This creates a white plate that covers all the areas your other colors will print on.
2
Underbase mode
Full image = white under everything. Use when all colors need the white base.

Key areas only = white only under the colored areas, not under the black outlines. This avoids a white halo showing around black ink.

Threshold = white only under bright/light colored areas.
3
Choke — preventing the white halo
When you print white then put color on top, if the white underbase is the same size as your artwork, a tiny white edge can show around the design (called a halo). Choke shrinks the white plate inward by a few mm so the white never sticks out from under the color. The choke circle diagram shows you how much you're shrinking it. Start at 0.5mm.
4
Trap — preventing gaps between colors
The opposite problem: if two colors are supposed to touch exactly, a tiny misalignment at the press can leave a gap where the garment color shows through. Trap slightly overlaps neighboring color plates so there's no gap even if registration is slightly off. 0.2mm is usually enough.

Channels — managing your print colors
How to select exactly which colors to print

After separation, the Channels panel shows every color the app detected. You control which ones get processed, exported, and printed.

ControlIconWhat it does
Eye button👁 / 🚫Shows or hides the channel in the preview. Does not affect export.
Print button🖨 (yellow = on)Includes or excludes from export and print. Turn OFF for colors you are not printing this run.
Color countText below listShows how many screens you are currently set up to print.
💡 Example — 4 color job You ran Spot separation and got 7 colors. You only want to print Red, Navy, Black, White UB. Tap the 🖨 button on Yellow, Cyan, Magenta and any others to turn them off. Now only 4 plates will export.
📋 Print order matters Always print in this order: White underbase first → lightest colors → darkest colors → black last. Flash cure between colors if using plastisol ink on dark garments.

Color isolation tool — tap any color
The best way to handle spot color artwork

The Color Isolation tool in the PMS tab lets you tap any color in your artwork and generate a clean plate for just that color. This gives you much more control than automatic separation.

1
Go to PMS tab → tap "🎯 Tap Color in Artwork"
The button turns orange and your artwork cursor becomes a crosshair. The app is waiting for you to tap a color.
2
Tap the color you want to isolate
Tap directly on the red feathers, or the navy band, or whatever color you want. A preview instantly appears showing what the app thinks belongs to that color.
3
Adjust the Tolerance slider
Tolerance controls how strict the color match is. Low (5–20) = only pixels very close to your tapped color. Good for clean solid colors.
High (50–100) = catches more shades of that color. Good for gradients and shadows within a color.
4
Edge clean and Expand
Edge clean removes stray isolated pixels outside your main selection — the noise and speckles. Set to 1–2 for clean crisp edges.

Expand grows your selection outward by pixels. Use +1 or +2 to add trap — ensures the color overlaps its neighbors slightly and no gap shows at the press.
5
Tap ⚡ Generate Plate
The full-resolution plate is created and added to your channels list. Repeat for every color. You can see all your isolated colors listed below with their names and hex codes.
✅ Best practice for spot color artwork Remove BG → tap each color one at a time → generate plates → turn off any auto-sep channels you don't need → export only your isolated color plates.

Presets — saving your favourite settings
Set it once, use it forever

Once you find settings that work well for a particular type of job, save them as a preset. Next time you open that type of job, load the preset and you're ready to go in one tap.

1
Dial in your settings
Set your LPI, shape, angle, choke, RIP mode, black mode, ink limit — everything the way you like it for this type of job.
2
Go to the ⭐ Presets tab → type a name → tap Save
Name it something useful like "Dark garment 4 color spot" or "DTF photo 35LPI". The preset saves everything including all toggles.
3
Load it next time
Tap any preset in the list and all settings load instantly. Built-in presets are always there as starting points. Your own presets are saved on this device.
💡 Suggested preset names "White garment spot 4col" · "Black garment with underbase" · "DTF photo simulation" · "Coarse mesh 25LPI" · "Fine detail 55LPI"

Exporting and printing your plates
Getting files off the app and onto film
A
Export All — downloads every active plate
Tap ⬇ Export All in the header. Only channels with the 🖨 print button active will download. Each file is named plate_[color].png and includes registration marks and the info label bar.
B
Download individual plates
Hover over any plate thumbnail at the bottom of the canvas. Two buttons appear: ⬇ DL downloads just that plate. 👁 View opens it full size in a new tab so you can zoom in and check quality.
C
Print Plates modal — for printing direct from Safari
Tap 🖨 Print in the header. Choose 1-up, 2-up, 4-up, or all-on-one layout. Then tap the print button and Safari's print dialog opens. You can print directly to an AirPrint printer or save as PDF.
D
Step & Repeat — ganging multiple copies
Tap ⊞ Step&Repeat. Set columns, rows, and gap size. This tiles your artwork multiple times on one sheet — useful for printing many small designs on one film to save material.
📋 What to do with the files Send your exported PNG files to an inkjet film printer (many print shops offer this service). They print on clear acetate film using black ink. You then use this film to expose your screen under UV light. The black blocks the UV, leaving the unexposed emulsion to wash away — creating your stencil.

Glossary of terms
Plain English definitions
AM Screening (Conventional halftone)
Regular grid of dots where dot size varies to create lighter/darker areas. Standard halftone method. Good for most screen printing jobs.
Choke
Shrinking the white underbase plate inward by a small amount (usually 0.3–1mm) to prevent a white halo showing around the edges of your design.
CMYK
Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key (Black). The four process ink colors that can be mixed by overlapping halftone dots to simulate full color. Used for photographic prints.
Color separation
The process of splitting a full-color design into individual single-color layers. Each layer becomes one screen in screen printing.
DPI (Dots Per Inch)
Resolution. How many ink dots fit in one inch. Higher = more detail but larger files. 300 DPI is standard for print. 600 DPI for fine detail.
DTF (Direct to Film)
A printing method where ink is printed directly onto a special film, powder is applied, it's heat cured, then heat transferred onto a garment. No screens needed.
Emulsion
The light-sensitive coating on a screen. When exposed to UV light it hardens. Unexposed areas wash away to create the stencil.
Film negative
A clear film with your design printed in black ink. Black blocks UV light during screen exposure, creating the stencil. One film per color.
FM Screening (Stochastic halftone)
Random placement of same-size dots where density varies to create lighter/darker areas. No regular grid = no moiré. Better for fine detail and DTF.
GCR (Grey Component Replacement)
Replaces equal amounts of CMY ink with K (black) ink in neutral/grey areas. Reduces total ink usage and improves black density.
Halftone
A pattern of dots that creates the illusion of continuous tones. Big dots = dark, small dots = light, no dots = white. The only way to print gradients with solid ink.
Ink limit / Total ink coverage
The maximum amount of ink allowed across all channels combined. DTF: 280%. Screen print: 300–320%. Exceeding this causes bleeding, poor adhesion, and cracking.
Knockout black
Black ink removes all other ink colors from the areas it covers. The black plate is the only thing printing in black areas. Required for DTF and dark garments.
LPI (Lines Per Inch)
How many rows of halftone dots fit in one inch. Higher LPI = finer, more detailed halftone but requires finer mesh screen. Rule: LPI ≤ mesh count ÷ 3.
Mesh count
The number of threads per inch in a screen printing mesh. Higher mesh = finer detail but less ink flow. Common: 86 (thick ink), 110–156 (standard), 200+ (fine detail).
Moiré pattern
An unwanted optical pattern that appears when two regular dot grids overlap at the wrong angle. Prevented by using standard CMYK screen angles (C:15°, M:75°, Y:90°, K:45°).
Overprint black
Black ink prints on top of all other ink colors, not replacing them. Creates richer blacks on white garments. Wrong choice for dark garments.
Pantone (PMS)
Pantone Matching System. A standardized color system used worldwide. Each color has a number (e.g. PMS 485 Red). Your ink supplier can mix any Pantone color exactly.
Platen
The flat board on a screen printing press that the garment sits on. It holds the garment flat and in the same position for each color pass.
Registration marks
Crosshair targets that appear on every plate in the same position. Used to align multiple screens so each color prints in the correct position relative to the others.
RIP (Raster Image Processor)
Software or process that converts artwork into a high-contrast print-ready format. Creates pure black/white output for film printing with no grey or anti-aliased edges.
Spot color
A specific pre-mixed ink color with its own dedicated screen. More vibrant than CMYK simulation. Each spot color = one screen. Best for logos and flat color artwork.
Squeegee
The rubber blade used to push ink through the screen stencil onto the garment during printing.
Trap
Slightly overlapping neighboring color plates (usually 0.1–0.3mm) to prevent gaps showing between colors if registration is slightly off at the press.
UCR (Under Color Removal)
More aggressive than GCR — removes CMY from shadow areas and replaces with K. Reduces total ink coverage. Use for dark garments or when ink limit is a concern.
Underbase
A white ink layer printed first on dark garments. Acts as a bright base for colors printed on top. Without it, dark garment color shows through and dulls your inks.

FAQ and troubleshooting
Common problems and how to fix them
My separated plates have dots and noise outside the artwork area
Fix: Tap ✂️ Remove BG before running separation. The background color is being caught by the separator. Remove it first and re-run separation.
The image is too small / too large on the canvas
Fix: Check your Canvas Size settings. Type the actual print width in cm (e.g. 30cm for a chest print). The image automatically fits the canvas. If it still looks wrong, tap Fit to Window to rescale the view.
The halftone dots are too big / too small
Fix: Adjust the LPI slider. Lower LPI = bigger dots. Higher LPI = smaller dots. Also check your mesh count — if your LPI is marked red in the mesh guide, your dots are too fine for your screen and will clog.
I can see a moiré pattern (wavy interference pattern)
Fix: Enable Per-channel angles toggle in the HT tab — this gives each color channel a different angle (C:15°, M:75°, Y:90°, K:45°) which prevents moiré. Also enable the Moiré prevention toggle.
The navy/dark blue is showing as black in my separation
Fix: Very dark colors get absorbed into the K (black) channel in CMYK separation. Use the 🎯 Color Isolation tool — tap the navy, set tolerance to 30–50, and generate a dedicated navy plate. Then turn off the auto-sep channels you don't need.
There's a white halo around my design on dark garments
Fix: Increase the Choke value under Underbase/Choke. Start at 0.5mm and increase until the halo disappears in the preview. The choke shrinks the white underbase plate so it can't peek out from under the color plates.
My DTF print is cracking or peeling
Fix: Your total ink coverage is too high. Go to the Ink tab, tap 📊 Analyse, check the total. It must be under 280% for DTF. Tap Apply Ink Limit to automatically reduce it.
The plate labels / info bar are not showing on my exported files
Fix: Make sure you are exporting via ⬇ DL on the plate thumbnail or ⬇ Export All. The labels are added during the download process. If viewing the plate preview on screen the label may appear small — it's sized for the full resolution output file.
Colors I turned off (🖨 off) are still appearing
Fix: The 👁 eye button only hides in preview. The 🖨 print button must be turned off (goes grey/dim) to exclude from export. Make sure you tapped the printer icon, not the eye icon.
My screens are not aligning — colors are off-register
Fix: This is a press setup issue, not an app issue. All your film plates have reg marks in exactly the same position. At the press, use registration pins or a light table to align each film plate before burning. Check that your platen is not moving between color passes.
The app is running slowly on my iPad
Fix: Set DPI to 150 while working, then switch to 300 or 600 for final export. Turn off Live Preview if you don't need it. For very large images, the separation and halftone processing takes more time — this is normal.
