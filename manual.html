What is screen printing?
The basics explained simply

Screen printing is a stencil system. One stencil per colour. Each stencil is a screen. You push ink through each screen onto the garment one colour at a time.

The simple versionYour design → split into colours → each colour printed on film → film burns a screen → ink pushed through screen → garment printed.
Your Design
→
Split into colours
→
Print on film
→
Burn screens
→
Print on garment
→
Done
1
One screen per colour
4-colour design = 4 screens. Each screen only prints one colour of ink.
2
Film negatives go on the screen
You print each colour as a black film negative. Press it against a light-sensitive screen under UV light. The black blocks the light. Wash out the unexposed areas. You now have a stencil.
3
Print one colour at a time
Garment on platen. Screen 1 down. Push ink with squeegee. Lift. Screen 2. Repeat. Registration marks keep all colours aligned perfectly.
What this app doesTakes your design and automatically creates each colour as a separate film-ready file with registration marks, plate labels, and all the settings a professional printer would use.

Job Setup Wizard — NEW in v5
The app sets everything up automatically before you start
✅ New in v5Every time you open the app, the Job Setup Wizard appears first. Answer 4 questions and the app configures all settings automatically — exactly like a professional printer would set up for that job type.
Q1
Garment colour — White/Light or Dark/Black?
White/Light: no underbase needed. App sets overprint black, standard settings.
Dark/Black: white underbase enabled automatically. App sets knockout black, choke 0.8mm.
Q2
Design type — Spot colors or Photo/Gradient?
Spot colors/Logo: flat countable colours like a badge or cartoon. App recommends Color Isolation tool.
Photo/Gradient: smooth blending like a photograph. App sets CMYK separation, ellipse halftone dots, medium GCR.
Q3
Print method — Screen Print or DTF?
Screen Print: film → burn screens → press. App sets 1-bit RIP, 320% ink limit.
DTF: direct to film transfer. App sets stochastic screening, 280% ink limit, mirror enabled.
Q4
How many colours?
Tells the app how complex the job is. Helps with recommendations for separation method and halftone settings.

Splitting your design into individual colour plates
⚡ Auto Sep — use for:
  • Photos and gradients
  • Smooth blending colours
  • Photorealistic artwork
  • Simulated process printing
🎨 Spot — quick detect:
  • Finds dominant colours automatically
  • Good starting point for logos
  • Can miss similar shades
  • Use Color Isolation after for cleanup

  • Any spot colour design
  • Tap exact colour you want
  • Cleanest result for screen printing

💡 Which method for your design?Can you count the colours? (Red, navy, black, white = 4) → Use 🎯 Color Isolation for each colour. Does it look like a photograph with gradients? → Use Auto Sep.
?
What is CMYK?
Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key (Black). Four ink colours that mix by overlapping halftone dots to recreate almost any colour. Auto Sep splits your design into these 4 channels.
?
What is a spot colour?
A pre-mixed specific ink — like actual red ink, not CMYK dots that simulate red. More vibrant. Each spot colour needs its own screen. 4 spot colours = 4 screens.
?
Black mode — what to choose
Knockout Black: removes other ink under black areas. Use for DTF and dark garments.
Overprint Black: black prints on top of everything. Use for screen print on white.
Pure K: only K channel carries black. Best for crisp text and thin lines.

Halftone
How gradients get printed with solid ink

Screen printing can only print ink or no ink. It cannot print 50% red. Halftone dots create the illusion of lighter or darker shades. Big dots = dark. Small dots = light. No dots = white.

Live preview in v5The 🔴 Live Preview toggle shows a real-time halftone preview as you drag sliders. Low resolution but instant. When you lift your finger, full quality processes automatically.
SettingWhat it doesStart here
LPILines per inch — how many dot rows per inch. Higher = finer detail but needs finer mesh.45 LPI
ShapeDot shape: circle, ellipse, square, diamond, line.Circle
AngleRotation of dot grid. Different channels use different angles to prevent moiré.Per-channel ON
ContrastPushes highlights lighter and shadows darker.20
Min/Max dotClips tiny dots that won't print and huge dots that merge.5% / 95%
AM vs FMAM = regular grid (conventional). FM = random dots (stochastic) — better for DTF and fine detail.AM for screen, FM for DTF
⚠️ Mesh count rule — criticalYour LPI must be no more than 1/3 of your mesh count. Example: 156 mesh screen = maximum 52 LPI. The mesh guide table in the app shows green/amber/red status automatically.
💡 When to skip halftoneIf your design is flat solid spot colours with no gradients — skip halftone entirely. Solid ink areas print as solid. Go straight to RIP.

RIP — creating your film negatives
The final step before burning screens

RIP = Raster Image Processor. Converts your artwork into pure black and white — maximum contrast — for printing on film.

✅ What good film output looks likePure solid black where ink should go. Pure white everywhere else. No grey. No soft edges. This burns the sharpest, cleanest screen.
ModeUse when
1-bit pure B&WSpot colours, logos, text, solid areas. Hardest edge.
Floyd-Steinberg ditherPhotographic prints without halftone. Random dot pattern.
Stochastic FMDTF, high detail photographic. Very fine random dots.
GrayscaleRarely needed. Only if your film printer handles greyscale.
A
Invert (Ink positive)
Normally film is black where ink goes. Some exposure units need the opposite — white where ink goes. Toggle Ink Positive if your bureau needs this. Ask your film output provider.
B
Mirror output
Some printers need film mirrored so ink side faces down against the screen during exposure. If burns come out reversed, turn Mirror on.
C
Sharpen passes
Runs an unsharp mask before 1-bit conversion. 1–2 passes gives crisp edges. Too many creates artifacts.

Registration marks
How multi-colour prints stay perfectly aligned
✅ Reg marks only appear on exported film — not on the preview canvasWhile you work, you see just a faint dashed border. Bold reg marks only appear on your downloaded film files where they belong.
1
8 reg marks on every exported plate
Corners and midpoints — 8 total. Always in exactly the same position on every plate. This is how you align screen 2 perfectly to screen 1 at the press.
2
Bold thick marks — visible on film
Solid black, thick crosshairs, large outer circle, centre dot. Clearly visible when you hold film up to the light.
3
Plate info bar at the bottom of every film
Every exported film has a solid black bar showing: colour name with swatch, LPI, angle, DPI, print size, and date. Top left corner label too. When you have 5 films on your workbench you always know which is which.
4
Using reg marks at the press
Tape your first film (underbase or black) to the screen. Expose it. When setting up, use reg marks as reference points. All other films must have their reg marks in exactly the same position. Use registration pins or a light table for precision.
💡 Test firstPrint a test sheet through all screens on paper before printing on garments. Hold to light — all reg marks should stack perfectly. If not, adjust registration before wasting garments.
11
Underbase and choke
Printing on dark garments
⚠️ Dark garment ruleColoured ink printed directly onto a black garment looks dull and wrong because the garment shows through. You must print white ink first. This is the underbase.
1
Enable white underbase
Toggle White Underbase ON in the left panel. A white plate is created covering the areas your colours will print on.
2
Underbase mode — three options
Full image: white under everything. Use when all colours need a white base.
Key areas only: white only under coloured areas, NOT under black outlines. Best for most designs — prevents white showing around black ink. The pro choice.
Threshold: white only under bright/light areas. Good for photographic designs.
3
Choke — prevents the white halo
When white underbase is the same size as your artwork, a tiny white edge can show around the design. Choke shrinks the white plate inward so it hides under the colour plates. The choke circle visual shows how much you are shrinking it. Start at 0.5mm. Dark garments use 0.8–1mm.
4
Trap — prevents gaps between colours
Slightly overlaps neighbouring colour plates (0.1–0.3mm) so no garment colour shows through if registration is slightly off at the press.
5
View underbase on canvas
Tap 👁 View Underbase on Canvas — the canvas switches to show just the white underbase plate. You can see exactly where white will print. Then use the layer buttons to add other colours on top to check coverage.

✅ New in v5 — full manual control over white underbaseSometimes the automatic underbase is not perfect. Maybe you want white only under specific light colours but not under dark shadow areas. The pencil editor lets you paint and erase the white underbase manually.

The brush toolbar appears. The canvas switches to underbase view. You can now draw on it.

🖌 Paint: adds white to the underbase. Draws white areas back in.
⬜ Erase: removes white from the underbase. Removes areas you don't want underbase under.
3
Brush controls
Size: brush radius in pixels. Larger for big areas, smaller for detail.
Hardness: how sharp the brush edge is. 80–100% for crisp edges, lower for soft feathering.
Opacity: how strongly each stroke applies. 100% for solid, lower for gradual buildup.
4
Apple Pencil pressure sensitivity
If you have an Apple Pencil, pressing harder makes the brush larger and more opaque. Light touch = subtle. Heavy press = full effect. Works automatically — no setup needed.
5

Your brush strokes are baked into the white underbase plate permanently. The edit canvas clears and the updated underbase is saved. You can then view other layers on top to check the result.

Glossary
Every term explained in plain English
AM Screening
Regular grid of dots where dot size varies to create lighter/darker areas. Standard halftone. Good for most screen printing.
Choke
Shrinking the white underbase plate inward (0.3–1mm) to prevent a white halo showing around design edges on dark garments.
CMYK
Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key (Black). Four process ink colours mixed by overlapping halftone dots to recreate full colour. Used for photographic prints.
Color isolation
Tapping a colour in your artwork to isolate it as a clean single-colour plate. The most accurate method for spot colour separation.
Color separation
Splitting a full-colour design into individual single-colour layers. Each layer becomes one screen.
DPI
Dots Per Inch. Resolution. Higher = more detail, larger files. 300 DPI = standard for print. 600 DPI = fine detail and RIP output.
DTF (Direct to Film)
Printing method where ink is printed onto special film, powder applied, heat cured, then heat transferred to garment. No screens needed.
Emulsion
Light-sensitive coating on a screen. UV light hardens it. Unexposed areas wash away to create the printing stencil.
Film negative
Clear film with design printed in black ink. Black blocks UV during screen exposure. One film per colour.
FM Screening (Stochastic)
Random placement of same-size dots where density varies to create lighter/darker areas. No regular grid = no moiré. Better for DTF and fine detail.
GCR (Grey Component Replacement)
Replaces equal amounts of CMY ink with K (black) in grey/neutral areas. Reduces total ink and improves black density.
Halftone
Pattern of dots creating illusion of continuous tones. Big dots = dark, small = light, none = white. Only way to print gradients with solid ink.
Ink limit / Total ink coverage
Maximum ink across all channels combined. DTF: 280%. Screen print: 300–320%. Exceeding causes bleeding and cracking.
Knockout black
Black ink removes all other ink from areas it covers. Required for DTF and dark garments. Prevents muddy colour mixing under black areas.
Layer
In v5, each separated colour is a layer. You can view, hide, edit, and control each colour independently — exactly like layers in Photoshop or Procreate.
LPI (Lines Per Inch)
How many rows of halftone dots per inch. Higher = finer, more detailed halftone but requires finer mesh. Rule: LPI ≤ mesh count ÷ 3.
Mesh count
Number of threads per inch in a screen. Higher = finer detail, less ink flow. Common: 86 (thick ink), 110–156 (standard), 200+ (fine detail).
Moiré pattern
Unwanted optical interference pattern when dot grids overlap at wrong angles. Prevented by using standard CMYK angles: C:15°, M:75°, Y:90°, K:45°.
Overprint black
Black prints on top of all other inks. Creates richer blacks on white garments. Wrong choice for dark garments.
Pantone (PMS)
Pantone Matching System. Standardised colour numbers used worldwide. Your ink supplier can mix any Pantone exactly. e.g. PMS 485 = standard red.
Platen
Flat board on the screen printing press that the garment sits on. Holds garment flat and in the same position for each colour pass.
Procedure Log
v5 feature. Saves settings plus your personal notes about a job — mesh used, ink type, what worked. Your recipe book for successful jobs.
Registration marks
Crosshair targets on every plate. Used to align multiple screens so each colour prints in the exact same position. In v5 these only appear on exported film, not on the working preview.
RIP (Raster Image Processor)
Converts artwork to pure black/white, maximum contrast, for film printing. No grey, no soft edges — just solid black where ink goes.
Spot colour
Pre-mixed specific ink with its own dedicated screen. More vibrant than CMYK simulation. Each spot colour = one screen.
Squeegee
Rubber blade used to push ink through the screen stencil onto the garment.
Trap
Slightly overlapping neighbouring colour plates (0.1–0.3mm) to prevent gaps between colours if press registration is slightly off.
Underbase
White ink layer printed first on dark garments. Acts as a bright base for colours on top. Without it, dark garment colour shows through and dulls inks.

FAQ and Troubleshooting
Common problems and exactly how to fix them
I lost my work when I tapped the Help button
Fix for next time: tap 💾 Save BEFORE tapping Help. The Help button opens the manual in a new tab, which may reload the app. Your .spv5 project file will restore everything. Always save before navigating away.
Noise and dots outside my artwork after separation
Fix: tap ✂️ Remove BG before running separation. The background colour is being caught by the separator. Remove it first then re-run separation.
The image is tiny / too small on the canvas
Fix: check Canvas Size — type your actual print width in cm (e.g. 30). Tap the 100% quick button. Tap Fit to Window. The image should fill the canvas correctly.
Dark navy blue is showing as black in my separation
Fix: very dark colours get absorbed into the K (black) channel in CMYK. Use the 🎯 Color Isolation tool — tap the navy area, set tolerance 30–50, generate a dedicated navy plate. Turn off the K channel or reduce it.
White halo showing around my design on dark garments
Fix: increase the Choke value (try 0.8–1mm). Also switch UB Mode to "Key areas only" which prevents white printing under black outlines.
I can see moiré patterns (wavy interference)
Fix: enable Per-channel angles in the HT tab — gives each channel a different angle (C:15°, M:75°, Y:90°, K:45°) which prevents moiré. Also ensure your LPI is not too high for your mesh.
Halftone dots are too big or too small
Fix: adjust the LPI slider. Lower LPI = bigger dots. Higher LPI = smaller dots. Check the mesh guide — if your LPI shows red, the dots are too fine for your screen and will clog.
DTF print is cracking or peeling after washing
Fix: total ink coverage is over 280%. Go to Ink tab → tap Analyse → check the total percentage. It must be under 280% for DTF. Tap Apply Ink Limit to reduce automatically.
A colour I turned off is still exporting
Fix: there are two buttons per layer. The 👁 eye only hides in preview. You must tap the 🖨 printer button (turns grey/dim) to exclude from export. Make sure you tapped the printer icon not the eye.
How do I edit the white underbase to remove it from shadow areas?
Fix: first set UB Mode to "Key areas only" — this automatically removes white from under black/dark areas. Then tap ✏️ Edit with Pencil/Finger. Choose ⬜ Erase mode. Draw over the areas where you don't want white. Tap ✓ Done Editing.
My screens are not aligning — colours are off-register
Fix: this is a press setup issue, not an app issue. All film plates have reg marks in exactly the same position. At the press, use registration pins or a light table to align each film before burning. Check your platen is not moving between colour passes.
The app is slow on my iPad when processing large images
Fix: set DPI to 150 while working, then switch to 300 or 600 for final export only. Turn off Live Preview if not needed. Large images at 600 DPI take more time to process — this is normal.
How do I save my settings so I don't have to redo them every time?
Two ways: Preset = tap ⭐ tab → type a name → Save Preset. Loads settings instantly next time. Procedure Log = same tab → type name and notes → Save to Procedure Log. Loads settings plus shows your notes about what worked.
Script error showing in Claude's preview window
This is a Claude sandbox restriction, not a bug in the app. The Claude preview window blocks certain JavaScript for security. When you run the app from GitHub Pages (your ta2hunter.github.io/Screenprint URL), no script errors occur. The app is fine.
